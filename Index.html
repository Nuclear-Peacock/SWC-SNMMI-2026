Why This Matters

AI is already reshaping nuclear medicine for physicians, physicists, and technologists alike. Understanding where we are on the curve — and where the hype ends — is the foundation of effective use.

Context

Applications
Four Domains of AI Use in Nuclear Medicine
▼
  • Clinical — SPECT/PET reconstruction, lesion detection, dosimetry support, report drafting
  • Educational — Board prep for physicians & physicists, technologist training, patient handouts, quiz creation
  • Research — Literature synthesis, protocol design, physics modeling, grant writing
  • Administrative — Prior auth letters, QC documentation, scheduling optimization, policy drafts
Ethics
The Challenge of Accuracy
▼

AI models hallucinate — they generate confident, plausible, but factually incorrect content. In nuclear medicine, this has real stakes.

  • Always verify AI output against primary sources
  • Bias in training data can skew clinical recommendations
  • AI-generated reports require radiologist sign-off
  • Patient data must never be entered into public LLMs
Note
Important Caution — All AI Tools
▼
No patient data — including images, reports, identifiers, or any protected health information — should be uploaded to or entered into any AI tool without explicit permission and in accordance with your institutional policies, BAA agreements, and applicable HIPAA regulations. This applies to all AI platforms, not only image and video tools.

02

Anatomy of a Prompt

Prompt engineering is the art and science of crafting precise instructions for AI models to generate desired responses. The better your prompt, the better your output.

Definition
Everyday Analogies
▼

You already know how to prompt — you just don't call it that:

  • A well-formed PubMed query
  • A PICOT research question
  • A structured patient history to a consultant
  • Precise search terms in a PACS system

Prompt engineering applies the same precision to generative AI.

Framework
The 6 Elements of an Effective Prompt
▼
Role
Who should the AI act as? (e.g., "You are a board-certified nuclear medicine physician...")
Task
What specific action should it perform? Clear verbs matter. (create, summarize, compare, draft)
Context
What background does the AI need? (patient scenario, audience, setting)
Example
Optional but powerful — provide a sample of what you want.
Format
How should the output be structured? (bullet list, table, paragraph, SOAP note)
Tone
What register? (formal/technical for colleagues, plain language for patients)
Worked Example — Nuclear Medicine Context
▼
Role
You are an experienced nuclear medicine physician and educator.
Task
Create a concise patient education handout about a whole-body bone scan.
Context
The patient is a 62-year-old with a new diagnosis of prostate cancer. They are anxious and have no prior imaging experience.
Format
Use plain language. Include: what to expect, radiation safety reassurance, preparation instructions, and what happens after.
Tone
Calm, reassuring, and clear. Avoid jargon. Use a 6th-grade reading level.
03

Prompt Techniques

Five techniques that dramatically improve AI output quality — applicable whether you are interpreting scans, writing protocols, or building training materials.

Technique 1
Free Text Prompting
▼

The foundation — available in every AI platform. Apply the 6-element anatomy and iterate.

  • Refine your prompt based on each output
  • Start a new chat if context becomes polluted
  • Rate responses and give explicit feedback
  • Use "forget history" where supported
Technique 2
Role-Playing Prompts
▼

Assign the AI a specific expert persona to improve accuracy and calibrate tone for your audience.

"You are a nuclear medicine physicist reviewing a center's SPECT/CT quality control protocol. Identify gaps relative to SNMMI guidelines..."
Technique 3
Chain-of-Thought Prompting
▼

Force the AI to reason step-by-step before reaching a conclusion. Critical for complex clinical and physics scenarios.

  • Ask AI to "explain your reasoning step-by-step"
  • Define each reasoning step explicitly
  • Best for differential diagnosis and dosimetry checks
Technique 4
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
▼

Ground AI output in your documents — not just its training data. Upload a source and prompt against it.

  • Upload SNMMI guidelines, review articles, or protocols
  • Generate study guides, summaries, or Q&A grounded in the source
  • Every claim is traceable back to your uploaded document
Technique 5
Custom AI Tools (GPTs & Gems)
▼

Build or use pre-configured AI assistants trained on specific instructions — no prompting from scratch each time.

  • Custom GPTs (ChatGPT) and Gems (Gemini) save reusable system prompts
  • Build a "Board Review Q&A Bot" or "Report Drafting Assistant"
  • Share within your department for consistent outputs
  • No coding required — configure via natural language
